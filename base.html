Overview

Problem Statement

Access to clean and safe drinking water is a fundamental right. As the number of natural and chemical disasters have increased, we’re seeing more contamination than ever in our waterways-- in the United States, about half of our water sources are too polluted for swimming, fishing, or drinking.
To make matters worse, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and other government entities have covered up the truth about water quality in numerous contamination hotspots across the country.

Different Areas of Water Contamination Incidences

Research

Ashland, MA

Between 1965 and 1978, Nyanza Inc and other companies disposed of toxic textile effluent in a 35-acre parcel of land designated as the Nyanza Chemical Waste Dump, leading to large quantities of industrial waste and contamination of soil, ground water, and wetlands with mercury, chromium, arsenic, lead and more.

For years, residents in the surrounding areas continued swimming, bathing, and drinking from local water sources, unaware of the contaminants that they were being exposed to. It wasn’t until fish began dying by the thousands, birds began falling out of the sky, and the city began seeing an unprecedented number of rare cancers appear that they started to question their water quality.

Kevin, only 26 years old, had died from one of the rare cancers that locals were being diagnosed with. It took Ashland residents eight years of begging for federal assistance before the government finally intervened. To no one’s surprise, toxic concentrations of mercury, chromium, arsenic, and lead were confirmed across numerous bodies of water and cleanup efforts began immediately.

36 years have passed since the EPA’s initial arrival in 1987, and residents have since been told that the water is safe... but what does “safe” really mean? And why did the EPA just receive another $20 million earlier this month to continue removing contaminated groundwater?

Competitive Analysis

Key Findings
  1. Existing solutions with high accuracy are expensive
  2. Testing textile dyes requires reagents that are highly toxic
  3. There are no consumer facing products connected to a decentralized network

Ideation + Design

Based on the research and the findings, I designed a water contamination sensor that immediately uploads test results to a decentralized virtual platform. There are three unique usecases that the sensor could be applied to.
